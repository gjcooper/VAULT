## My Thoughts

This paper investigates the relationship between the selection of strategies, and how people learn which strategy is best suited for a multi-attribute task. Given a series of choices where there were multiple strategies that gave equal payoffs (specifically a [[Take the Best]] strategy vs a routine of choosing the most successful option), would participants learn to routinely apply one of the possible strategies. This was checked in a subsequent test phase where the payoffs for each of the two decision methods (TTB vs OPT) were manipulated such that one was very successful and one was not successful at all.

In their first experiment successful selection of the winning option approached ceiling over the learning blocks, as well as a reduction in the numbers of attribute acquisitions (measured via reveals with [[Mouselab]]). With minal feedback in exp 1 (Correct vs incorrect) most people selected the [[Take the Best|TTB]] strategy.

However in a follow up study with a manipulation of the feedback to highlight either the winning option (symbol next to the winner) or the winning option and predictive attributes (red square around option + attributes) the number of people using a strategy of just selecting the best option (without regard for attribute information) increased significantly. This led to a call for the integration of [[attention]] effects in any model that incorporates [[reinforcement learning]] in the acquisition of the routine application of strategies in multi-attribute choice.
