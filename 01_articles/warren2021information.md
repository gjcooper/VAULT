## My Thoughts

A treatise on the side of embodied cognition, pushing back on the idea of [[Bayesian brain]] view, or more explicitly the constructionist view that peoples perception is driven by some explicit interaction of their prior belief about the world interacting with their sense input.

Instead [[William H. Warren]] states that perception is more the process of interactions between an agent (animal/person) and an environment. For example peoples (and bees/birds etc) ability to navigate narrow openings can be explained by looking at higher order features on the environment and how we interact with it, like [[optic flow]] as a person moves through said environment. This optic flow can help navigate , for instance shown to keep bees in the centre of a corridor.

The end of the paper struck me as really railing against the [[Active Inference]] mob, where everything is a Bayesian model. While there are some things here I like, I think this whole approach goes to far in the other direction - **there is no model**.

My personal beliefs are that we are biological systems that at time are better represented by a model if we want to understand how parts of our cognition works, however there maybe could be a more ecological underpinning of at least the perception part of psychology that grounds our understanding in the higher order feature of the environment that we are tuned into, as opposed to focusing solely on lower order stimulus features that we then construct our reality from.
